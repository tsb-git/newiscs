Pandemic and Climate Change

The COVID-19 outbreak was named a pandemic on the 11th of March, 2020. It has ever since caused wreckage all around the world, leading to a catastrophe not only in the health sector but also in the economic and social arena. At one end, where it is causing havoc in human activities, it is serving as a boon for nature and environment, healing climate and weather conditions.

The worsening health conditions and the rapid spread of this vicious virus coerced governments worldwide to impose lockdown, to adopt the strategy of “flattening the curve.” The whole World has been on an unprecedented lockdown along with adopting various precautionary measures from personal to national levels. These measures have had their impact on climate and air quality, whereas the environment and climate in return could affect the spread of SARS-CoV-2, the virus leading to COVID-19. The intertwined relationship among environment, climate change and public health is exemplified in the pandemic of COVID-19.

A few studies reported significantly improved air quality and visibility in many cities and postulated or attributed that to the reduced emissions from industrial productions, local transport, power generations and many other economic activities.

While the cleaner air during lockdown has demonstrated that it is feasible for human society to improve air quality, the climate implications of reduced aerosol and gaseous pollutants emissions during the global pandemic of COVID-19 remain to be scrutinized.

According to recent studies as the human economic activities(industries, trade & commerce, transport and aviation, etc.) came to a standstill and human mobility reduced, there was substantial amount of change in the quality of air, which was observed in countries such as Spain and Italy. In China the NO2 levels reduced drastically over the lockdown. From February 10th to 25th, 2020, it was observed that the NO2 levels had reduced up to 20-30 %. In cities all over the world the concentration of NO2 in air had dropped drastically (from 5.6 μg/m3 to 0.2 μg/m3), as a result of ceased transportation.

It has been observed that there was a decrease in air pollution in the Southwest US cities, with a decrease of 31% in prime cities such as Los Angeles and 25% in San Diego.

In some cities of India an increment in ozone concentration was observed due to a drastic reduction in NO2 by 80.18%. Due to the restrictions in traffic and dialled down factory productions, industrial states of India such as Gujarat experienced a drastic reduction in air pollutants over March 25 to April 20, 2020.

The recession of transportation and industries during the lockdown decreased the carbon emissions worldwide(NASA 2020). The predictions made by experts suggests that this decline in anthropogenic CO2 emission, is the most colossal after World - War II. During the lockdown, global air traffic has seemed to reduce by 60% which has led to a temporary dip in carbon emissions. As reported by scientists at Columbia University, 5-10% reduction in carbon emission was observed in Italy within a week of lockdown from the 14th to 20th March, 2020.

Decarbonization has been suggested by climate scientists and environmental protection activists as a solution to global climate change. Since the emission reduction during COVID-19 lockdown is of a global scale, it could offer some clues about the efficacy of greenhouse gases and PM2.5 reductions of a hypothetical global scale decarbonization. A recent preliminary calculation showed a 17% cut of daily global carbon dioxide emissions in April 2020 compared with the 2019 average

Fighting against COVID-19 has provided humankind an enlightening lesson, which has established a database and experience for developing future strategies to combat global pandemics, improve air quality and mitigate climate change. In the battle against the pandemic, it is obvious that the combined effort of academic institutions, medical institutions, private corporates, governments and general public is vital in order to bring the pandemic under control. This is also applicable in global air quality improvement and climate change mitigation. Particularly, academic researchers of relevant areas should participate in providing advice to policy makers in formulating science-based decisions, assessing risk and issuing early warning and contribute to raising general public awareness through education and communication.
