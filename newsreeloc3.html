Maritime Security: New era of International Cooperation

Ocean has always been a common heritage since time immemorial. In recent times, when the world has been witnessing a complex era, the maritime security has emerged as the big challenge. Few challenges like the maritime dispute, natural disaster etc have dramatically changed on the issue of maritime security in recent times.

From Tradition to life-line

As India has the benefit of a long coast-line since ancient days, ocean and maritime security has been a part of her tradition. Buddhism and Hinduism spread to South-East by the sea-route. Ocean has remained as the joint heritage. Now, keeping in mind the complex changes in international arena, a stable maritime territory has emerged as the most essential thing. The factors like technology, trade and trust are the key ingredients to the stable maritime territory. India’s celebrated policy SAGAR [Security and Growth for all in the region] has caught the attention of the world as it outlines a farsighted policy framework.

The issue of natural disaster and proper mitigation which saves a large number of human lives require information sharing, mutual capacity building etc. So, technology and cooperation at highest level can make the maritime security a life-saver. The other critical issue has been the threat from the non-state actors in the ocean. Apart from the efficient services rendered by the Indian Navy, the state-of the art White Shipping Information Fusion Centre have enhanced India’s image and stature as a responsible and trust-worthy security provider.

In the era of Industrial Revolution 4.0, the knowledge-based economy and technology hold the key for sustainable prosperity. The Prime Minister Mr Modi’s call for a unimpeded trade and commerce with the help of high-end technology will pave the path for sustainable growth. It is pertinent to mention that 90% of global trade is routed on the sea.1 As the sea happens to be the most cost-effective mode of transport, maritime security and up-gradation of maritime infrastructure are the essential ingredients. The other logical imperative from the maritime security is the ‘unimpeded security’. It requires a multi-lateral cooperation being supported by the high-end technology. The entire world economy is thriving on cooperative and free trade. Free trade without unnecessary restrictions, high duties and trade friendly economic environment facilitate growth oriented economic activities leading to mutual prosperity. Similarly, in maritime trade also ‘free trade’ without unnecessary restriction can usher in mutual prosperity. The sustainable mutual prosperity is the foundation of a strong maritime which can be a life-line.

From mare clausum to mare liberum

The principle of Mareliberum which was conceptualized by Dutch philosopher Hugo Grotius in 1609 has once again gained currency in recent times. According to Grotius, ‘…..sea is common to all, because it is so limitless that it can not become a possession of any one, and because it is adapted for the use of all, whether we consider it from the point of view of navigation or of fisheries.’

Ensuring the freedom of navigation and unrestricted trade in maritime have become the responsibility of all countries. Apart from cooperation of all, maritime laws, trade laws, international laws related to navigation and supportive economic regime are essential policy imperatives expected from all countries. In the era of globalized world and free flow of information supported by advanced technology, the archaic concept of Mareclausm or closed sea and restricted trade will rupture the flow of sustainable mutual prosperity. This aspect has

also been clearly stressed by Prime Minister Mr Modi in his speech at the united Nations Security Council High- Level open debate on Enhancing Maritime Security on August 9, 2021. It clearly highlights India’s highest priority to maritime security and free trade for a brighter maritime prosperity. To ensure the mare-liberum, strong policy framework to ensure maritime security, maritime trade and mutual prosperity is needed.

Maritime trade and security require solid and strong infrastructure. To remain viable in ever-changing global economic scenario, enhancement of absorption capacity of existing infrastructure along with the creation of new infrastructure of maritime connectivity are the twin challenges that have to be addressed at the earliest. A well calibrated world class norms and standard on creation of futuristic infrastructure must be provided priority.

Challenges ahead:

In the era of Industrial Revolution 4.0, sweeping changes induced by political upheavals, economic reforms and technological advancement are going to impact maritime. The emergence and potential of blue economy along with enhancement of trade will shape the future of maritime prosperity. Technology is also changing rapidly. Adoption and implementation of technology enabled infrastructure will pave the way for rapid transformation. A skilled man-power on maritime trade, infrastructure and security is another immediate requirement.

Trade operates on mutual trust and mutual benefits. Similarly in maritime security trust will ensure mutual prosperity. One can see the benefit of mutual trust and trade in ancient times on the stone engravings on the Konark temple wherein a de-boarding of a giraffe from ship owned by Odia merchants. It clearly shows the robust and prospering trade between Indian traders with far off places like Africa and other countries. The ancient merchants of sea stressed on mutual trust, mutual benefits and a shared prosperity.

Free maritime trade with robust infrastructure and dispute settlement mechanism will have a great impact on the lives of common persons. A new era of maritime can be ushered in with clear policy outline and roadmap for shared prosperity.
